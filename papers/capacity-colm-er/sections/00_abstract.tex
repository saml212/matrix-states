How many independent bindings does a fixed-size fast-weight state hold
before recall collapses? We measure this directly in a single-layer
DeltaNet trained on a synthetic associative-recall grammar, grading
recovery by exact continuous cosine similarity rather than argmax over a
codebook, and we report three results and one provisioning law. First, at
state dimension $d_{\mathrm{state}}=64$ the held-out 4-hop recall frontier is
a sharp transition, not a graceful decline: a logistic fit over the measured
loads locates the midpoint at $K/d_{\mathrm{state}} = 0.5455$ (95\%
bootstrap CI $[0.5385, 0.5513]$). % evidence: C2
Second, the ratio is not the law: the identical $K/d_{\mathrm{state}}$
window is flat at ceiling at $d_{\mathrm{state}}=128$ (12 of 12 cells at
$h4=1.0$), % evidence: C3
the $d_{\mathrm{state}}=80$ frontier sits at $0.6779$ (CI
$[0.6683, 0.6867]$), excluding a pre-registered ratio-invariance band,
% evidence: C10
and $d_{\mathrm{state}}=96$ shows no transition anywhere through
$K/d_{\mathrm{state}}=0.9375$. % evidence: C11
Between the two located frontiers, capacity in raw bindings grows as a
two-point $d^{1.97}$ exponent (CI-propagated range $[1.86, 2.09]$):
consistent with state bytes, not state width. % evidence: C15
Third, the leading single-variable account of the growth, anchor-table
coherence, fails a direct test: frozen coherence injected at and above the
collapsing scale's own realized training band leaves recall at exact
ceiling in 19 of 19 cells, under both concentrated and diffuse injection
structures. % evidence: C6
Fixed-size states are the constant-memory endpoint of the KV-cache
trade-off; these measurements give their capacity a located,
pre-registered frontier.
